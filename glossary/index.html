Key Terms in Jainism

Philosophy & Categories(21)

Ajīva

अजीव

Non-soul. Everything that is not conscious: matter (pudgala), space, time, motion-medium (dharma), rest-medium (adharma).

See also:JīvaPudgalaDravya

Anekāntavāda

अनेकान्तवाद

The doctrine of multiplicity of viewpoints. Reality has infinite aspects; any single perspective gives only a partial view. Built-in epistemic humility.

One of Jainism's most distinctive contributions to world philosophy. Counsels against dogmatism by recognizing that any statement is true only "in some respect." Closely related to syādvāda.

See also:SyādvādaNaya

Āsrava

आस्रव

Influx — the inflow of karmic matter into the soul because of the soul's own activity (mind, speech, body). Third of the seven tattvas.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 6.1
See also:KarmaBandhaSaṁvara

Bandha

बन्ध

Bondage — the actual binding of karma to the soul. Fourth of the seven tattvas.

See also:KarmaĀsravaSaṁvaraNirjarā

Dravya

द्रव्य

Substance. Jain ontology recognizes six eternal dravyas: jīva (soul), pudgala (matter), dharma (motion-medium), adharma (rest-medium), ākāśa (space), kāla (time).

All six are uncreated, indestructible, and eternal. None was made by anything else. There is no creator — see Foundation 03.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 5.1–5.4 · Foundation 03
See also:JīvaPudgalaTattva

Ghāti-karma

घाति-कर्म

The four "destructive" karmas that obscure the soul's essential capacities: jnāna-āvaraṇīya, darśana-āvaraṇīya, mohanīya, antarāya. Their destruction produces kevala-jnana (omniscience).

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 10.1
See also:KarmaKevala-jnānaArihant

Jīva

जीव

Soul. The conscious principle. Eternal, indestructible, infinite in number. The first of the seven tattvas.

Every living being has a jīva — humans, animals, microscopic life, even tiny souls in plants, water, fire, earth. The jīva's natural state is infinite knowledge, perception, bliss, and energy; it is currently obscured by karma.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.4 · Samayasara
See also:AjīvaTattvaParamātmāJnāyaka

Jnāna

ज्ञान

Knowledge. Five types in Jain philosophy: mati (sensory), śruta (scriptural), avadhi (clairvoyant), manaḥparyaya (mind-reading), kevala (omniscient).

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.9
See also:Kevala-jnānaPramāṇa

Jnāyaka

ज्ञायक

The "Knower" — the soul as pure knowing-principle. Kundakunda's preferred term in the Samayasara for the soul seen from the absolute (niścaya) standpoint.

Cited in: Samayasara, gatha 7
See also:JīvaParamātmā

Karma

कर्म

In Jain technical usage, karma is a substance — extremely subtle particles of matter (pudgala) that bind to the soul because of how the soul acts. NOT a moral force or a vague law.

Eight major types: jnāna-āvaraṇīya (knowledge-obscuring), darśana-āvaraṇīya (perception-obscuring), vedanīya (feeling), mohanīya (deluding), āyu (lifespan), nāma (body), gotra (status), antarāya (obstruction). Each obscures a specific capacity of the soul.

Not: "What goes around comes around" — that is the casual usage; Jain karma is much more specific.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 6.1–6.5, 8.4–8.14 · Foundation 02
See also:Ghāti-karmaĀsravaBandhaSaṁvaraNirjarā

Kevala-jnāna

केवलज्ञान

Omniscient knowledge. Direct, complete, simultaneous knowledge of all substances in all their modes across all three times. The fifth and highest type of knowledge.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.9, 10.1
See also:KevaliJnānaArihant

Mokṣa

मोक्ष

Liberation. Complete freedom from all karma. The soul, freed, attains its pure nature and goes to Siddhaloka. Seventh and final tattva.

Not: "Salvation" — Christian theological baggage that doesn't apply.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 10.1, 10.7
See also:SiddhaSiddhalokaSaṁvaraNirjarā

Naya

नय

Perspectival standpoint — partial but valid view of a thing. Distinct from pramāṇa, which is full means of knowledge. Foundation of anekāntavāda.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.6 · Samayasara — niścaya/vyavahāra
See also:AnekāntavādaSyādvādaPramāṇa

Nirjarā

निर्जरा

Shedding — the gradual removal of accumulated karma through austerity (tapas) and purification. The second half of the path to liberation. Sixth tattva.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 9.3
See also:SaṁvaraMokṣaTapas / Tapasyā

Paramāṇu

परमाणु

The smallest indivisible particle of matter (pudgala) in Jain physics. Different from but conceptually parallel to the modern atom.

See also:Pudgala

Paramātmā

परमात्मा

The supreme soul — in Jain usage, the soul as it is in its perfect (Siddha) state. Not a creator god; the realized form of any soul.

See also:SiddhaJīva

Pramāṇa

प्रमाण

Means of valid knowledge. Two categories in Jain epistemology: parokṣa (indirect — sensory and scriptural) and pratyakṣa (direct — clairvoyant, mind-reading, omniscient).

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.6, 1.10
See also:NayaJnāna

Pudgala

पुद्गल

Matter. The only one of the six dravyas with form (rupa). Includes karmic matter (subtle particles that bind to the soul).

See also:ParamāṇuKarmaDravya

Saṁvara

संवर

Stoppage — halting the inflow of new karma. The first half of the path to liberation. Fifth of the seven tattvas.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 9.1
See also:NirjarāĀsravaMokṣa

Syādvāda

स्याद्वाद

The doctrine of conditional predication — every statement about reality is qualified by "in some respect." Often paired with anekāntavāda.

See also:AnekāntavādaNaya

Tattva

तत्त्व

Category of reality. Jain philosophy holds seven tattvas: jīva, ajīva, āsrava, bandha, saṁvara, nirjarā, mokṣa.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 1.4
See also:JīvaAjīvaĀsravaBandhaSaṁvaraNirjarāMokṣa

Beings & People(11)

Acharya

आचार्य

The head of a monastic order; a senior monk who guides others on the path. Authors of foundational texts (Kundakunda, Umaswami, etc.) hold this title.

See also:Muni / SadhuUpadhyaya

Arihant

अरिहन्त

One who has destroyed the four ghati karmas (knowledge-obscuring, perception-obscuring, deluding, obstructing) and attained omniscience while still embodied. The first being venerated in the Navkar Mantra.

Cited in: Navkar Mantra · Tattvartha Sutra 10.1
See also:SiddhaKevaliTirthankaraGhāti-karma

Aryika

आर्यिका

A female ascetic in the Digambara tradition, wearing a single white sari (unlike Digambara male monks who are unclothed). Held to teaching role at Upadhyaya level.

Gaṇadhara

गणधर

A chief disciple of a Tirthankara, who organized the teaching into the canonical scriptures. Mahavir had 11 gaṇadharas, the foremost being Gautama (Indrabhuti).

See also:ĀgamaTirthankara

Jina

जिन

"Conqueror" — one who has conquered the inner enemies (passions, ignorance). All Tirthankaras are Jinas; not all Jinas are Tirthankaras.

See also:TirthankaraArihant

Kevali

केवली

One who has attained kevala-jnana (omniscient knowledge). All Arihants are Kevalis; Tirthankaras are a special subset who establish the sangha.

See also:Kevala-jnānaArihantTirthankara

Muni / Sadhu

मुनि / साधु

A Jain monk who has taken the five great vows (mahavrata). The fifth category venerated in the Navkar Mantra.

See also:MahāvrataShravak / Shravika

Shravak / Shravika

श्रावक / श्राविका

Lay follower (male / female). Takes the twelve vows (anuvrata + gunavrata + shikshavrata) appropriate to a householder.

See also:AṇuvrataMahāvrata

Siddha

सिद्ध

A fully liberated soul. After the Arihant's physical death, all karma is shed; the soul ascends to Siddhaloka at the apex of the universe and remains there forever in pure consciousness.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 10.5–10.7
See also:ArihantMokṣaSiddhaloka

Tirthankara

तीर्थंकर

A "ford-maker" — a soul who, in their final birth, attained complete liberation through their own effort and established the four-fold sangha. There are 24 Tirthankaras per time-cycle.

Not a god, not a deity, not a creator. A Tirthankara is a jiva who, having destroyed the four ghati karmas, attained kevala-jnana (omniscience) and then the Siddha state. They cannot intervene; they are exemplars.

Not: god, deity, avatar, prophet

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 10.1, 10.5–10.7 · Foundation 01
See also:JinaArihantSiddhaKevaliSamavasaraṇa

Upadhyaya

उपाध्याय

A teacher-monk; one who instructs other monks in the scriptures. The fourth being venerated in the Navkar Mantra.

See also:AcharyaMuni / Sadhu

Cosmology & Iconography(6)

Aṣṭa-mahā-prātihārya

अष्टमहाप्रातिहार्य

The eight great signs that mark a Tirthankara during the samavasarana: aśoka tree, simhāsana (lion-throne), cāmara (fly-whisks), three umbrellas, golden lotuses underfoot, dundubhi (divine drum), puṣpa-vṛṣṭi (rain of flowers), bhāmaṇḍala (halo).

Cited in: Bhaktamara Stotra v.28–32, 45–47
See also:SamavasaraṇaTirthankara

Divya-dhvani

दिव्यध्वनि

The "divine voice" of a Tirthankara during the samavasarana — speech that arises spontaneously and is heard by every being in their own language. A unique attribute in Digambara doctrine.

Cited in: Bhaktamara Stotra v.48
See also:SamavasaraṇaTirthankara

Lāñchana

लाञ्छन

The identifying symbol associated with each Tirthankara, depicted at the base of their murti. Bull for Rishabhanath, lion for Mahavir, snake for Parshvanath, etc.

See also:TirthankaraMūrti

Samavasaraṇa

समवसरण

The divine assembly hall manifested for a Tirthankara's first sermon after attaining kevala-jnana. The eight prātihāryas surround the Tirthankara during the assembly.

See also:TirthankaraAṣṭa-mahā-prātihāryaDivya-dhvani

Saṁsāra

संसार

The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth in which souls (jīvas) wander, driven by their karma, until liberation. Often metaphorized as the "ocean of becoming."

See also:MokṣaKarmaJīva

Siddhaloka

सिद्धलोक

The realm at the apex of the universe where liberated souls (Siddhas) reside in pure consciousness, beyond rebirth.

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 10.5–10.7
See also:SiddhaMokṣa

Practice & Ritual(9)

Bhakti

भक्ति

Devotion. In Jain context: profound respect for one who has shown what is possible (the Tirthankara), and the willingness to walk in their footsteps. Closer to apprenticeship than worship.

See also:TirthankaraPūjā

Kāyotsarga

कायोत्सर्ग

Literally "abandoning the body" — standing meditation practice in which the practitioner stands motionless, focused inward. Also one of the two iconographic postures of the Tirthankaras (the other being padmāsana).

See also:Ṣaṭ-āvaśyakaPadmāsana

Mūrti

मूर्ति

A sacred sculptural representation of a Tirthankara. Used reverentially as object of veneration.

Not: "Idol" — pejorative English connotation that doesn't capture the role of murti in Jain practice.

See also:PūjāTirthankara

Padmāsana

पद्मासन

The lotus-seated meditative posture. One of the two iconographic postures of the Tirthankaras.

See also:Kāyotsarga

Pratikramaṇa

प्रतिक्रमण

The introspective ritual of "turning back" — review of conduct, confession of transgressions, renewal of vows. One of the six daily duties (ṣaṭ-āvaśyaka). Performed daily, fortnightly, and annually.

Combines self-examination, formal confession in canonical verses, and recommitment to ethical practice. Central to Jain spiritual life — the practical mechanism for stopping new karmic inflow (saṁvara) and shedding old karma (nirjarā).

Cited in: Tattvartha Sutra 9.20 (six āvaśyakas)
See also:SaṁvaraNirjarāṢaṭ-āvaśyaka

Pūjā

पूजा

Veneration ritual. In Jain context, pūjā honors the Tirthankara as an example to follow — not as a deity granting requests. See Foundation 01 for the distinction.

See also:MūrtiBhaktiTirthankara

Sāmāyika

सामायिक

The practice of equanimity — typically a 48-minute meditative period in which the practitioner sits in calm awareness, free of attachments and aversions. First of the six āvaśyakas.

See also:Ṣaṭ-āvaśyakaPratikramaṇa

Ṣaṭ-āvaśyaka

षडावश्यक

The six essential daily duties of a serious Jain practitioner: sāmāyika (equanimity), caturviṁśati-stava (praise of the 24 Tirthankaras), vandanā (veneration of acharyas), pratikramaṇa (introspection), kāyotsarga (standing meditation), pratyākhyāna (renunciation/vows).

See also:PratikramaṇaKāyotsargaSāmāyika

Tapas / Tapasyā

तप / तपस्या

Austerity, ascetic practice. Central to nirjarā — the shedding of accumulated karma. External (fasting, isolation) and internal (study, meditation) forms.

See also:NirjarāPratikramaṇa

Ethics & Vows(4)

Ahiṁsā

अहिंसा

Non-violence in thought, speech, and action. The first and foundational vow. Extends to all life forms — including microscopic life. Far more than vegetarianism.

See also:MahāvrataAparigraha

Aṇuvrata

अणुव्रत

The "lesser vows" taken by lay followers — partial versions of the five mahāvratas suited to householder life.

See also:MahāvrataShravak / Shravika

Aparigraha

अपरिग्रह

Non-attachment, non-possessiveness. One of the five great vows. Critique of how possessions distort the mind, not just minimalism.

See also:Mahāvrata

Mahāvrata

महाव्रत

The five great vows taken by monks/nuns: ahiṁsā (non-violence), satya (truth), asteya (non-stealing), brahmacarya (celibacy), aparigraha (non-attachment).

See also:AhiṁsāAparigrahaAṇuvrataMuni / Sadhu

Texts & Traditions(8)

Āgama

आगम

The canonical scriptures of Jainism — what the Tirthankaras taught, transmitted through the gaṇadharas (chief disciples). Digambara and Shvetambara differ on which texts constitute the surviving canon.

See also:Gaṇadhara

Bhaṭṭāraka

भट्टारक

A Digambara religious-administrative figure heading a peeth (seat) and overseeing temple administration. Bispanthi accepts Bhattaraka authority; Terapanthi (Digambara) does not.

See also:DigambaraBispanthi / Terapanthi (Digambara)

Bispanthi / Terapanthi (Digambara)

बीसपंथी / तेरापंथी

Two sub-traditions within Digambara. Bispanthi uses wet substances in pūjā and accepts Bhaṭṭāraka authority; Terapanthi (Digambara) uses only dry aṣṭadravya, rejects subordinate-deity worship and Bhaṭṭāraka authority over monks. Note: unrelated to the Shvetambara Terapanthi.

See also:DigambaraBhaṭṭāraka

Digambara

दिगम्बर

"Sky-clad" — the tradition where male monks practice complete nudity as the visible expression of total aparigraha. Holds that final mokṣa requires the digambara state. Strong in Karnataka, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Bundelkhand.

This library follows the Digambara tradition. The Sanskrit/Prakrit texts, translations, and interpretive framings come from this lineage.

Not: "Sect" — use "tradition" or "sampradāya"

See also:ŚvetāmbaraBhaṭṭāraka

Saṅgha

संघ

The four-fold Jain community: monks (sādhu), nuns (sādhvī / aryikā), laymen (śrāvaka), laywomen (śrāvikā). Established by every Tirthankara at the start of their teaching.

See also:Muni / SadhuAryikaShravak / Shravika

Stotra

स्तोत्र

A hymn of praise, typically composed in verse. Bhaktamara Stotra and Kalyana Mandir Stotra are canonical examples.

See also:Bhakti

Sūtra

सूत्र

An aphoristic text — concise, dense Sanskrit (or Prakrit) statement intended to be memorized and unpacked through commentary. Tattvartha Sutra is the foundational example.

Śvetāmbara

श्वेताम्बर

"White-clad" — the tradition where monks and nuns wear white robes. Holds that women can attain mokṣa and that Mahavir married. Strong in Gujarat and Rajasthan.

See also:Digambara
