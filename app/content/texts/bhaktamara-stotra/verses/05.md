translation_en: 'O Lord of Sages! Even so, drawn by devotion to You, I — though without strength — am still driven to compose Your praise. Out of love, does not a doe charge at a lion to protect her own fawn, without weighing her own strength?'

meaning_en: |
  Verse 5 closes the opening sequence with one of the most beloved images in the entire stotra: a doe charging a lion to protect her young.

  The image works because it inverts what we expect. Lions hunt deer; deer flee lions. That is the natural order. But love disrupts that order. A doe with a fawn behind her does not calculate. She does not consider her chances. She charges — knowing she will likely die — because the alternative is unbearable.

  Manatunga places himself there. He has no chance of describing the Jina adequately. Indra has not done it. Brihaspati could not. But *bhakti-vaśān* — drawn by devotion — he attempts anyway. The doe at the lion is the structure of his entire enterprise.

  This is *bhakti* not as feeling, but as force. Something that overrides calculation. Something that does the impossible because the alternative — *not attempting* — is what feels impossible.

The first four verses of the Bhaktamara establish a pattern: I am not Indra, I am not Brihaspati, I cannot grasp the moon's reflection, I cannot swim the cosmic ocean. By verse 5, the question becomes urgent: *then why are you doing this?*

Manatunga's answer is the doe.

A doe knows she is smaller than a lion. She knows she will lose. She knows the lion can kill her in seconds. None of this matters when her fawn is behind her. *Prītyātma-vīryam-avicārya* — without weighing her own strength against love.

This is the line that anchors the entire stotra. Bhakti is not the claim of equality with what one praises. It is not even the rational decision to praise. It is the force that makes you praise *despite* knowing you cannot do it justice. The doe does not strategize against the lion. She charges because she has no choice — her love makes the calculation impossible.

For a modern reader, this is one of the few moments in religious literature where the text honestly names what devotion is at the level of feeling, not theology. *I cannot do this. I am doing it anyway.* Every parent who has done something dangerous for a child knows this exactly. Every artist who has tried what was beyond their craft knows it. Every person who has loved something more than they understood it knows it.

That is why Manatunga is writing. Not because he can. Because love does not let him not.
