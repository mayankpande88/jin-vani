meaning_en: |
  Verse 10 contains one of the most distinctive teachings of Jainism, stated almost in passing.

  *Tulyā bhavanti bhavataḥ.* "They become equal to You."

  Read that again. Manatunga is saying — *and the verse treats it as obvious* — that the devotee who praises the Jina with sincere qualities becomes equal to the Jina. Not similar. Equal. *Tulyā.*

  This is not metaphor. This is the foundational soteriology of Jainism. Every soul (*jiva*) has within it the same potential as a Tirthankara. Liberation is not a special status reserved for some. It is the default destiny of every soul that successfully purifies itself. The Tirthankara is just one who has done it. The praiser, by attending to those qualities and embodying them, is doing the same work.

  The closing rhetorical question — *what master does not raise his dependents to his own level?* — is gentle, almost humorous. But it contains a serious point. In many religious traditions, the difference between deity and devotee is permanent. In Jainism, the deity is just a soul who got there first. The devotee is on the same path, working at it.

  This is, theologically, an unusually optimistic claim. And it is stated here as if it required no argument. Of course you become equal to what you sincerely contemplate. What else would happen?

Verse 10 of the Bhaktamara contains, almost in passing, what may be the most distinctive teaching of the Jain tradition.

*Tulyā bhavanti bhavataḥ.* "They become equal to You."

A serious devotee, attending to the qualities of the Tirthankara, becomes equal to the Tirthankara. Not "becomes blessed by." Not "earns a place near." Equal.

In most religious traditions, the gap between divinity and devotee is permanent. The deity is one kind of being; the devotee is another kind. The relationship may include grace, salvation, blessing — but ontologically, there are two categories.

Jainism does not have two categories. There are only souls — *jivas* — and they all have the same potential. A Tirthankara is a soul who has fully realized that potential. A devotee is a soul who has not yet. The work is the same; the difference is only how far along.

This is why the closing line treats the claim as obvious. *What master in this world does not raise his dependents to his own level?* The implication: of course. That is what real teachers do. A musician who teaches well does not keep students forever beneath them — they teach the student to become a musician too. The Tirthankara is the master who, through example, lifts the dependent to the master's level.

For a modern reader, especially one shaped by the assumption that religion is mostly about a permanent gap between human and divine, this verse is genuinely subversive. The Jina's example does not exist to be admired from below. It exists to be matched. The praise is not flattery. It is the slow work of becoming.

You become what you contemplate. You are working on it whether you know it or not.
