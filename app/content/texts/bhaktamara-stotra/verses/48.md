translation_en: 'Sought after by those searching for the road to heaven and to liberation; supreme alone in declaring the truth of *sad-dharma*; reaching the three worlds with vivid meaning; equipped with the property of naturally transforming into every language — such is Your divine voice (*divya-dhvani*).'

meaning_en: 'Verse 48 — the final verse of the Digambara Bhaktamara — describes the *divya-dhvani*, the unique form of speech attributed to a Tirthankara. In Digambara tradition, the Tirthankara&apos;s voice does not arise from physical articulation; it emanates and is heard by every being in its own language simultaneously. This is one of the most distinctive teachings of Digambara doctrine. The verse closes the stotra by naming this miracle: the Tirthankara&apos;s teaching reaches the three worlds, in every language, on its own.\n\nThe stotra ends here. Forty-eight verses; forty-eight chains. The poem that the tradition says broke a prison has now made the same circuit through the reader&apos;s mind: from bowing (verse 1), through humility (2-10), praise (11-33), refuge from fear (34-43), the poet&apos;s signature (44), and the eight prātihāryas of the *samavasarana* (45-48). What it leaves the reader with is what verse 1 began with: a way of standing in relation to the Jina that asks nothing and receives everything.'

The forty-eighth verse. The final chain.

Forty-eight verses; forty-eight chains. The stotra that the legend says freed Acharya Manatunga from prison has now traveled, in the reader&apos;s mind, through the same circuit. Bow. Humility. Praise. Refuge. Signature. Assembly.

What it leaves the reader with is what verse 1 began with: a way of standing in relation to the Jina that asks nothing — and receives everything.
