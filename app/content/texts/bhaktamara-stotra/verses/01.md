The first verse of the Bhaktamara is a *mangalacharana* — an opening invocation. It begins with a bow.

In a generation taught to "be your own hero," opening with reverence for someone else's example can feel uncomfortable. Doesn't this just reinforce the hierarchies we're trying to escape?

But notice what the verse does **not** do.

The speaker doesn't ask Adinath for anything. No wealth, no protection, no favor, no afterlife reward. The verse simply acknowledges: someone walked this path before me. Their teaching is the only refuge in the ocean of worldly life.

> This is closer to a scientist citing a predecessor than a believer praying. *Standing on the shoulders of giants.*

On this point the texts are clear: a Tirthankara is not a savior. Acharya Kundakunda's *Samayasara* teaches that the soul is its own doer (*karta*) and its own enjoyer (*bhokta*) — liberation comes through the soul's own purification, not from an outside agent. Acharya Pujyapada's *Sarvarthasiddhi* calls the Tirthankara the *avalambana* — the support, the example — not the giver of moksha.

What the Tirthankara is: an example. He is what is possible. Bowing acknowledges that another human being reached liberation through their own effort, and that the same path is open to anyone willing to walk it.

In Jainism, *bhakti* — devotion — is profound respect for someone who showed what is possible, and the willingness to walk in their footsteps. The Bhaktamara itself is the demonstration: across all 48 verses, Manatunga praises Adinath's qualities and asks for nothing. No request, no petition, no plea. The structure is recognition, not solicitation.

For a modern reader, perhaps this is the surprising part: a tradition often called "religious" with no creator god, no salvation through prayer, no intermediary between you and what you could become. Just you, your effort, and the example of those who went before.

That is what the first bow of the Bhaktamara is.
