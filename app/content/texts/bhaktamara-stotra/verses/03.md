translation_en: 'O You whose footstool is worshipped even by the wise (devas)! Without wisdom, having abandoned all shame, I am ready to praise You. Who but a child would suddenly wish to grasp the moon&apos;s reflection floating in water?'

meaning_en: |
  Verse 3 deepens the humility of verse 2 with a memorable image: a child reaching into water to grasp the reflection of the moon.

  The image works on several levels. First, it acknowledges Manatunga's own *aparipakvatā* — his unripeness, his lack of full wisdom — by comparing himself to a child. Second, it concedes the impossibility of the task: the moon's reflection cannot be grasped. Third, it pivots: only a child *attempts* such a thing, because only a child does not yet know it cannot be done.

  *Vigata-trapaḥ* — "having lost all shame" — is the key compound. It is not boastfulness; it is the willingness to attempt something one knows is beyond one's capacity, simply because the attempt itself is honoring.

Verse 3 of the Bhaktamara is one of the most-quoted in the entire stotra. The image is unforgettable: a child seeing the moon&apos;s reflection in a pool of water and reaching to grasp it.

The reflection is real — in a sense. It glows. It moves. It has all the visual qualities of the moon. But of course, it cannot be held. Anyone who has tried to scoop water disturbs the very thing they were reaching for. The moon scatters into ripples and remains where it always was: in the sky, untouched.

Only a child reaches for it. An adult, who knows the science of reflection, who has tried before and failed, will not bother. *Vigata-trapaḥ* — "without shame" — only a child still has that.

Manatunga places himself there. Not pretending to be Indra, not pretending to have the wisdom. Just: a child reaching for the moon. Knowing it cannot be grasped. Reaching anyway.

For a modern reader, this is one of the most accessible images in the stotra. We have all done this — reached for something we knew was beyond us, simply because we wanted to. The first poem, the first song, the first attempt at any art. We knew our reach was beyond our grasp. We reached anyway.

That is the gesture of *bhakti* in this verse. Not the achievement, but the willingness to try. The Jina is the moon — perfect, distant, untouchable. The reaching is the prayer.
