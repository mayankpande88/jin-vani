meaning_en: |
  Verse 7 marks a turn in the stotra. Verses 1–6 were about the speaker's smallness; verse 7 is the first statement of the Jina's effect.

  The image is one of the most powerful in the entire stotra: night to dawn. *Ali-nīla* — "blue as the bee" — describes the darkness; *kṣaṇāt* — "in a moment" — describes its disappearance. The sun does not negotiate with the night. The sun does not ask the night to leave. It rises, and the night is simply over.

  Manatunga is making a specific theological claim, and it deserves careful unpacking. He is *not* saying: "praise the Jina and your wishes will be granted." He is saying that the *act* of contemplating the Jina's qualities — *tvat-saṁstavena* — is what destroys karma. The Tirthankara does nothing. The praise is the practice.

  This aligns with what we saw in Foundation 01: the Jina is exemplar, not savior. The destruction of karma happens through the seeker's own act of orientation toward what the Jina represents — pure consciousness, freedom from passion, complete knowledge. The sun is not granting the dawn. The sun is the dawn. The praise is not asking for liberation. The praise is liberation in miniature.

Verse 7 of the Bhaktamara is one of the most-quoted verses in the entire stotra. It is the first verse where Manatunga states what the praise *does*.

Read it carefully. The verse does not say: *if you ask the Jina, your sins will be forgiven*. That is not the structure. The verse says: *the act of praising — saṁstavena — destroys the bonded karma*.

This is a specific theological position. In many religious traditions, sin is forgiven by an outside agent — a deity who decides to pardon. Jainism is different. There is no one to grant pardon. There is also no need for one. The contemplation of pure consciousness *is* the dissolution of impure consciousness, the way the rising sun *is* the end of night. The sun does not have to do anything to dispel the night. Its presence is the dispelling.

So when a Jain recites the Bhaktamara, what is actually happening? The verses describe the Jina's qualities — calmness, clarity, freedom from passion, complete knowledge. The reciter, through long-form attention to those qualities, slowly orients their own mind toward what the qualities represent. That orientation is itself the karma-shedding. The Jina does not arrive. The reciter quietly becomes more like the Jina by sitting with the Jina's qualities for a while.

For a modern reader skeptical of "prayers that work," this is the framing that matters. Verse 7 is not saying prayers are magic. It is saying that the long, repeated attention to certain qualities reshapes the mind that pays attention. The science of contemplative practice — well-studied now — would call this neuroplasticity. Manatunga calls it the sun rising.
