translation_en: 'Knowledge is of five kinds: *mati* (sensory cognition), *śruta* (scriptural knowledge), *avadhi* (clairvoyance — perception across distance), *manaḥparyaya* (knowledge of others'' mental modes), and *kevala* (omniscient knowledge).'

meaning_en: |
  This is one of the most-cited sutras in Jain philosophy. The five-fold classification of knowledge is foundational.

  - **Mati-jnāna** — knowledge through senses and mind. Everyone has this.
  - **Śruta-jnāna** — knowledge through scriptures, teaching, language. Available through study.
  - **Avadhi-jnāna** — clairvoyant perception, bounded but extending beyond ordinary senses. Devas have it innately; certain ascetics develop it.
  - **Manaḥ-paryaya-jnāna** — knowledge of the mental modes of other beings. Available to highly advanced ascetics.
  - **Kevala-jnāna** — complete, omniscient knowledge. The knowledge of a Tirthankara or any *kevali*. Direct, unmediated, simultaneous knowledge of all substances in all their modes across all three times.

  Crucially: the first two are *parokṣa* (indirect — they go through senses or symbols). The last three are *pratyakṣa* (direct — the soul knows directly without mediation). And only *kevala-jnāna* is complete.

  The path of liberation is, in part, the path from indirect knowledge to direct knowledge — culminating in *kevala* at full enlightenment.
