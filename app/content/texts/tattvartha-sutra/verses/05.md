translation_en: 'The (categories) are analyzed (*nyāsa*, classification / placement) from the standpoints of *nāma* (name), *sthāpanā* (representation), *dravya* (substance), and *bhāva* (mode/state).'

meaning_en: |
  This sutra introduces the *nikṣepa* method — a fourfold analytic technique unique to Jain philosophy. Any concept can be examined from four standpoints:

  - **Nāma** — by name. ("Jina" as a label given to anything called by that name.)
  - **Sthāpanā** — by representation. (A statue or image used to represent.)
  - **Dravya** — by substance. (The actual physical/material thing.)
  - **Bhāva** — by present state. (The thing in its current mode.)

  Take "Jina." A *nāma-Jina* is anyone named "Jina." A *sthāpanā-Jina* is a murti. A *dravya-Jina* is a soul that will become a Jina in a future birth. A *bhāva-Jina* is a soul actually in the state of being a Jina now.

  This is more than terminology. It enables Jain philosophers to discuss subtle distinctions without confusion — "*we mean Jina in the bhāva sense, not the dravya sense*." Modern philosophy of language took a long time to articulate similar distinctions; Jainism had this analytic apparatus from at least the 2nd century CE.
