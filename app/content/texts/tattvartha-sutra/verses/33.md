translation_en: 'In viparyaya (wrong cognition), there is no distinction between *sat* (real) and *asat* (unreal); cognition is haphazard (*yadṛcchā*), like that of a madman (*unmatta*).'

meaning_en: |
  The closing sutra of Chapter 1 explains what *viparyaya* (wrong knowledge) actually consists of, with a memorable comparison.

  An unmattaka — a deranged person — may still see, hear, and remember things. The cognitions are not absent; they happen. But the person cannot distinguish *sat* (what is real) from *asat* (what is not). Their cognitions are *yadṛcchā* — haphazard, undirected by truth-conditions. The light is on, but the discriminator has failed.

  This is the structure of viparyaya for *kumati*, *kuśruta*, and *vibhaṅga*. The objects are real, but the cognizer&apos;s relationship to them is so distorted by mithyā-darśana that the categories of reality (the seven tattvas — sutra 1.4) cannot be correctly distinguished.

  This is also why Jain philosophy treats *samyag-darśana* — right perception — as foundational. Without it, even accurate cognitions become wrongly held. The path of liberation begins with the mind that can tell *sat* from *asat*.

The closing sutra of Chapter 1. Wrong knowledge is not the absence of knowledge — it is knowledge whose discriminator has failed. The light is on; the categorizer is broken.
