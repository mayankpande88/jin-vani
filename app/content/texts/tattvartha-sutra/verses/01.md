meaning_en: |
  This is the first and most famous sutra of the Tattvartha — the entire Jain darśana in one line.

  Note the singular: *mokṣa-mārgaḥ* (the path), not *mokṣa-mārgāḥ* (paths). Three things, one path. Faith without knowledge is blind; knowledge without conduct is sterile; conduct without faith and knowledge is mere routine. Together they constitute the single path; separately, none of them does.

  Acharya Pujyapada's *Sarvarthasiddhi* opens with extensive analysis of why all three must be present and why the order — *darśana → jnāna → cāritra* — is significant. Faith comes first because without conviction, knowledge will not be sought; knowledge comes second because without it, conduct cannot be guided correctly.

The first sutra of the Tattvartha is the entire Jain darśana in one line.

The path is one. Faith, knowledge, conduct. Take any one away and the path collapses. Faith without knowledge becomes superstition. Knowledge without conduct becomes scholarship. Conduct without faith becomes habit. Only together do they make the way out.

Worth noticing: the sutra gives no role to a creator god, no role to grace, no role to ritual mediation. The path is internal — it is what *you* do with faith, knowledge, and conduct. Liberation is not granted; it is constituted. By you. Step by step.

For a modern reader, this is the most secular-looking opening any major religious text has. *The path to liberation is: be sure of what is true, know what is true, and act in accordance with it.* That sentence would be at home in a stoic manual or a science textbook. The specific *Jain* content — what the truths are, what right conduct looks like — is filled in across the rest of the Tattvartha. But the *form* of the path is pre-doctrinal. It is just: form a clear view, know it well, and live by it.
