translation_en: |
  I bow to the Arihants — those who have destroyed the inner enemies of attachment, aversion, and delusion.
  I bow to the Siddhas — fully liberated souls residing in Siddhaloka.
  I bow to the Acharyas — heads of monastic orders, guides of the path.
  I bow to the Upadhyayas — teacher-monks who instruct in scripture.
  I bow to all sadhus throughout the universe — those who have taken up the path of renunciation.

meaning_en: |
  The Navkar Mantra is veneration of the *pancha-paramesthi* — the five worthy ones. It does not name any single deity or Tirthankara. Instead, it identifies five categories of beings, two of which have completed the path (Arihants, Siddhas) and three of which are still walking it (Acharyas, Upadhyayas, sadhus).

  The mantra asks for nothing. It is pure recognition: this is what is possible. These are those who have done it or are doing it.

  The order is significant. Arihants are listed first — the embodied liberated, who can still teach. Siddhas, fully disembodied, follow. Then the three categories of those still on the path. The structure honors both attainment and effort.

The Navkar is the first mantra a Digambara child is taught. There is a reason for that.

In a tradition without a creator god, there is no obvious "default" prayer. Most religions begin their day by addressing a deity. Jainism does not have one to address.

What it has instead is the *pancha-paramesthi* — five categories of beings worth recognizing. The Navkar is the daily reminder: here is what is possible (Arihants, Siddhas), and here are those walking the path (Acharyas, Upadhyayas, sadhus). The mantra positions you in relation to a tradition, not in front of a deity.

Notice especially what is *not* in the mantra. No name of any specific Tirthankara. No name of Mahavir. No name of Adinath. The Navkar precedes all of them. It is not "Mahavir's prayer." It is the structural prayer of the tradition itself, and it would be valid in any time, on any continent, in any age.

For a young person asking "what does Jainism actually pray to?" the Navkar is the most honest answer: not to anyone. With everyone who has ever shown the way.
