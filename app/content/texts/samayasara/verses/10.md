translation_en: 'Bondage is caused by the passions (*kaṣāyas*), not by mere activity (*yoga*) alone. For one without passion (*akaṣāyī*), continuous activity is karma-shedding, not karma-binding.'

meaning_en: |
  The seventh chapter (Bandha-adhikāra, gathas 203–249) makes a precise distinction in the analysis of karmic bondage.

  Of the five āsrava-causes (mithyātva, avirati, pramāda, kaṣāya, yoga), Kundakunda identifies *kaṣāya* — the passions — as the operative cause of bondage. Without kaṣāya, no real bondage occurs. The other four operate through the kaṣāyas; the kaṣāyas are the engine.

  This explains a doctrinal puzzle: how can a kevali (omniscient one) act in the world — speak, walk, give sermons — if all activity binds karma? The answer: a kevali has destroyed mohanīya (the deluding karma that produces kaṣāya). Their activity continues, but with no passion driving it, no bondage results. Only a single moment of bare *īryāpatha* (path-of-walking) āsrava arises, which is shed in the next instant.

  For the practitioner, this is operative guidance: the work is not to stop *acting*. The work is to act *without passion*. The yogi works in the world; only the work-driven-by-greed/anger/pride binds karma.
