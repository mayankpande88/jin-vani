translation_en: 'The supreme reality is itself the *samaya* (the pure soul) — that which the Kevali, the Muni, the Knower, are. Munis abiding in that (very) nature attain *nirvāṇa*.'

meaning_en: |
  Toward the end of the Samayasara, Kundakunda offers one of his most distilled statements: the ultimate reality, *paramārtha-samaya*, is the pure soul itself. Not a god, not a metaphysical principle external to consciousness, not a creator. Just the soul, in its true nature.

  And the path to *nirvāṇa* is, simply, abiding in that nature. The *muni* who succeeds in resting in their own true nature — not in body, not in passion, not in karma, not in circumstance — attains liberation. Not because some external being grants it. Because the soul has finally returned to itself.

  This is the closing arc of Kundakunda&apos;s philosophy. Begin with the Siddhas (gatha 1). Distinguish the soul&apos;s own state from the alien state (gatha 2). Use the two standpoints (gatha 7). Recognize what doership actually means (gatha 73). And in the end (here): rest in your own nature, and that *is* nirvāṇa.

  Liberation, in this account, is not departure from the world but recognition of what was always already so. The soul has been pure all along. The path is the long process of remembering it.

  *Note:* This gatha summarizes the closing teaching of the Samayasara (around gathas 412–415 in standard editions). Exact placement to be verified by reviewer.
