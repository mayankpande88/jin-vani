translation_en: 'Just as iron chains bind, golden chains likewise bind. Just as bad conduct binds the soul, so too good conduct binds the soul.'

meaning_en: |
  This is the most-quoted teaching from the *Puṇya-pāpa* chapter (gathas 96–144) — and one of the more counterintuitive moves in Jain philosophy.

  Common moral thinking treats merit (*puṇya*) as good and demerit (*pāpa*) as bad — and assumes the path is to accumulate merit and avoid demerit. Kundakunda says: that&apos;s incomplete. Both bind karma. The merit-soul is in golden chains; the demerit-soul is in iron chains. The chains are different in appearance, different in their immediate effects (good rebirths vs. bad rebirths), but they are both *chains*.

  Liberation requires the cessation of *both*. The soul must move beyond merit and demerit alike — not by becoming amoral, but by acting from the soul&apos;s pure nature rather than from passion-driven calculation about good or bad results.

  This is theologically demanding. It says: even your good deeds, if done with attachment to their fruits, accumulate karma. Liberation is not the maximization of good karma; it is the transcendence of the entire karma-binding mechanism.

The third chapter of the Samayasara delivers the verse that surprises every modern reader: even your good deeds bind you, if they bind you. Liberation is not heaping up merit. It is moving beyond the entire merit-and-demerit transaction.
