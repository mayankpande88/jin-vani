The *Samayasara* — composed in Prakrit by Acharya Kundakunda — is the most influential philosophical text in the Digambara tradition. Its 415 gathas describe the nature of the soul (*jīva*) — what it truly is, how it becomes entangled in karma, and how it can return to its pure state.

This library presents 12 selected gathas from the Samayasara, chosen to represent the structure of the text — at least one from each of its 9 chapters (*adhikaras*). The full 415-gatha text is part of a future expansion.

**The 9 chapters:**

1. **Jīva-ajīva-adhikāra** (gathas 1–37) — the soul vs. non-soul distinction, the foundational ontology
2. **Kartṛ-karma-adhikāra** (gathas 38–95) — the soul as doer of its own karma
3. **Puṇya-pāpa-adhikāra** (gathas 96–144) — merit and demerit; both bind, despite different effects
4. **Āsrava-adhikāra** (gathas 145–164) — the influx of karma into the soul
5. **Saṁvara-adhikāra** (gathas 165–179) — stoppage of new karmic inflow
6. **Nirjarā-adhikāra** (gathas 180–202) — shedding of accumulated karma
7. **Bandha-adhikāra** (gathas 203–249) — the actual binding of karma
8. **Mokṣa-adhikāra** (gathas 250–287) — liberation as destruction of all karma
9. **Sarva-viśuddhi-jnāna-adhikāra** (gathas 288–415) — the all-pure knowing of the kevali

The 12 gathas selected here aim to give a reader a structural sense of the text rather than exhaustive coverage.

**Note on Prakrit:** Samayasara is in Prakrit (*Shauraseni Prakrit*), not Sanskrit. The transliteration uses standard Prakrit conventions; readers familiar with Sanskrit will find some grammar and vocabulary slightly different.

**Important:** Specific gatha numbers and the exact Prakrit text follow the Digambara editorial tradition; for less-canonical gathas, the text in this library represents the chapter&apos;s core teaching and may differ from a specific edition&apos;s exact wording. 4:T432,Acharya Kundakunda (~2nd century CE, sometimes earlier) is the towering figure of Digambara Jain philosophy. His works — especially the *Samayasara*, *Pravachansara*, *Niyamasara*, and *Panchastikaya Sangraha* — are quoted constantly in every later Digambara commentary. Bhattaraka tradition, monastic education, and lay study all return to Kundakunda repeatedly.

Of his works, the *Samayasara* — &ldquo;the essence of the soul&rdquo; — is the most influential. In 415 Prakrit gathas, it lays out the entire Digambara philosophy of the *jīva* (soul): what the soul truly is, how it gets entangled with karma, and how it returns to its pure nature.

Kundakunda is famous for his use of the *niścaya-vyavahāra* distinction — separating the absolute viewpoint (the soul as it really is) from the conventional viewpoint (the soul as it appears entangled in body and karma). This methodological move shapes nearly all subsequent Digambara philosophy.

Almost nothing is known of Kundakunda&apos;s historical life. The texts survive; the tradition has carried them.
5:T490,This gatha addresses one of the deepest issues in Jain philosophy: the question of *kartṛtva* (doership).

How does the soul get karmically bound? Through false identification. The soul, forgetting its own nature, comes to think *I am the body*, *I am the anger*, *I am this circumstance*. This is *para-samaya* (the soul in the alien state, from gatha 2). With each such identification, the soul "does" the karma of that identification — and karma binds.

The path back: see clearly that you are the soul, the Knower, not the body or the anger or the circumstance. When *kartṛtva* is correctly placed — on the soul itself, not on what is other — the binding stops. The soul becomes the *niścaya-kartā* (the absolute doer of itself, not the conventional doer of external things).

This is the heart of Kundakunda&apos;s teaching on liberation. Liberation is not the soul becoming something it is not. It is the soul stopping being what it is not.
