3:T46a,The most important sutra of the chapter, philosophically. Kevala-jnāna — the omniscient knowledge of a kevali — has no scope-limitation. Every substance (all six dravyas — soul, matter, motion-medium, rest-medium, space, time), in every mode (every past, present, and future state), is known directly and simultaneously.

This is what *sarvajnatva* (omniscience) means in Jain doctrine — and it is unusually rigorous. Not "knows a lot." Not "knows what is needed." Knows *all dravyas in all paryāyas*. The kevali&apos;s knowing has no gaps.

Compare this to the limitations of the lower jnānas:
- mati and śruta: all dravyas, but not all paryāyas (sutra 1.27)
- avadhi: only rūpī things (sutra 1.28)
- manaḥparyaya: an infinitesimal fraction of avadhi&apos;s range (sutra 1.29)
- **kevala: all dravyas in all paryāyas** — no limit

This is what the path culminates in. Foundation 03 (no creator god) becomes more comprehensible here: in Jainism, omniscience is the natural state of any soul whose obstructing karma has been destroyed. It is not divine; it is what consciousness *is*, when nothing obscures it.
