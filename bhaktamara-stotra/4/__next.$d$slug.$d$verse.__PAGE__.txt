3:T45d,Manatunga was a Digambara *Acharya* — a senior monk who had taken the full vows and reached teaching authority within the *sangha*. We have no surviving biography from his own time. What we know comes from later traditions, which differ in their details.

What we do have is the **Bhaktamara Stotra itself**. Forty-eight verses of remarkable Sanskrit poetry, dense with imagery, technically masterful, devotionally profound. The kind of text that suggests deep training in classical Sanskrit poetics combined with deep meditative practice.

The famous legend — bound in 48 chains by a king, freed by 48 verses — comes from later sources and varies across traditions. Whether it is historical fact, devotional tradition, or sacred metaphor is something each reader can decide for themselves. What is undeniable: for over a thousand years, this stotra has been recited daily across the Digambara world, in unbroken living tradition.

When you read these verses, you are reading words that have been repeated continuously for at least 1300 years. Not in a museum. As living recitation. *That itself is something.*

Verse 4 of the Bhaktamara is the verse that fully establishes the structure of the opening invocation. Verses 1, 2, 3 — bow, acknowledge predecessors, admit limits. Verse 4 — admit the impossibility entirely, with one of the most vivid images in Sanskrit devotional literature.

The Jina's qualities are an ocean. Not a small or peaceful ocean — *the* ocean. And not the calm sea of an ordinary day, but the ocean at the end of an age, churned by the cosmic storm-winds of dissolution, with crocodiles being lifted up by the waves themselves.

Who could swim across that — with bare arms?

The answer the verse forces you toward is: *no one*. Not even Brihaspati, the teacher of the gods. Not even the wisest mind classical Sanskrit literature can imagine.

And yet — Manatunga is doing exactly that. The stotra he is composing *is* the swim. Not because he claims to be capable. Because he knows he is not, and chooses to swim anyway.

This is one of the most precise statements of *bhakti* available in any literature. Devotion is not the claim of equality with what one praises. It is the willingness to attempt what cannot be done, because the One being praised deserves nothing less than the attempt.
