23:T4fd,Manatunga was a Jain *Acharya* — a senior monk who had taken the full vows and reached teaching authority within the *sangha*. We have no surviving biography from his own time. What we know comes from later traditions, which differ in their details — including his sectarian affiliation, claimed by both the Digambara and Shvetambara lineages. The library presents the 48-verse Digambara recension of the stotra.

What we do have is the **Bhaktamara Stotra itself**. Forty-eight verses of remarkable Sanskrit poetry, dense with imagery, technically masterful, devotionally profound. The kind of text that suggests deep training in classical Sanskrit poetics combined with deep meditative practice.

The famous legend — bound in 48 chains by a king, freed by 48 verses — comes from later sources and varies across traditions. Whether it is historical fact, devotional tradition, or sacred metaphor is something each reader can decide for themselves. What is undeniable: for over a thousand years, this stotra has been recited daily across the Digambara world, in unbroken living tradition.

When you read these verses, you are reading words that have been repeated continuously for at least 1300 years. Not in a museum. As living recitation. *That itself is something.*
24:T4fb,Verse 9 is theologically dense in a way easy to miss because the imagery is so beautiful.

Look at the structure. *Āstāṁ* — "let it stand aside" — sets up a *kim cha* (how much more) argument. Manatunga is saying: forget the full stotra; even *speaking of* the Jina (*tvat-saṁkathā*) destroys karma. He is making the bar smaller, not larger. Not "if you recite all 48 verses perfectly," but "if you mention the Jina's name."

The image of the sun is more than decoration. It addresses something a Jain reader might silently wonder: *if the Tirthankara is in Siddhaloka and cannot intervene, how does praise reach him?* The verse answers: praise does not need to reach him. The sun does not need to come to the lotus. Its rays — its *prabhā* — do the work from a distance. The lotus opens because the sun is what the sun is.

This is precise. Liberation in Jainism is not transactional. The Tirthankara's "qualities" — calmness, knowledge, freedom from passion — exist independent of any devotee thinking about them. But the devotee, by mentioning, by remembering, by recalling, gets *some of the light*. Not because the Jina sends it. Because the qualities themselves are like sunlight — wherever attention falls on them, the lotus opens a little more.
25:T713,
This is one of the verses that moves slowly past you on first reading and then opens entirely on the second.

The image: the sun is far away. The lotus is in a pond on the earth. They are nowhere near each other. And yet the sun's rays are sufficient — without any contact, without any closeness — to make the lotus open.

Now read the verse again. The Jina is in Siddhaloka, fully liberated, beyond rebirth, beyond any contact with this world. We are here, in *samsara*, with karma we cannot quite shake. The Jina cannot come to us. The Jina cannot intervene. So how does anything from the Jina reach us?

The verse answers without seeming to answer. *Dūre* — "far away" — and yet the radiance does the work. The sun does not need to come close to the lotus. Its qualities — its light, its warmth — function across distance. Likewise, the Jina's qualities function whether or not the Jina is near. They are real qualities; they can be attended to. Whoever attends to them gets some of the light.

This is, quietly, one of the most precise theological moves in the stotra. It explains how a non-theistic tradition can still have devotion that *does something*. The Tirthankara is not a deity reaching down. The Tirthankara is a sun, far away, whose qualities of light and warmth happen to be enough.

For a modern reader, this maps remarkably well to how influence actually works. The thinkers who shaped you most are usually not people who knew you. You read their books, watched their films, listened to their music. They never came close. Their *qualities* did the work — their clarity, their courage, their care. Across distance, sometimes across centuries, those qualities open something in you.

That is the structure of Jain devotion. The lotus opens. The sun, far away, is what does it.
