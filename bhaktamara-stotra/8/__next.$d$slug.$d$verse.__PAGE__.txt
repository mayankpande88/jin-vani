3:T4fd,Manatunga was a Jain *Acharya* — a senior monk who had taken the full vows and reached teaching authority within the *sangha*. We have no surviving biography from his own time. What we know comes from later traditions, which differ in their details — including his sectarian affiliation, claimed by both the Digambara and Shvetambara lineages. The library presents the 48-verse Digambara recension of the stotra.

What we do have is the **Bhaktamara Stotra itself**. Forty-eight verses of remarkable Sanskrit poetry, dense with imagery, technically masterful, devotionally profound. The kind of text that suggests deep training in classical Sanskrit poetics combined with deep meditative practice.

The famous legend — bound in 48 chains by a king, freed by 48 verses — comes from later sources and varies across traditions. Whether it is historical fact, devotional tradition, or sacred metaphor is something each reader can decide for themselves. What is undeniable: for over a thousand years, this stotra has been recited daily across the Digambara world, in unbroken living tradition.

When you read these verses, you are reading words that have been repeated continuously for at least 1300 years. Not in a museum. As living recitation. *That itself is something.*
4:T43e,Verse 8 is the *transition* verse of the stotra. After seven verses of self-deprecation and explanation, Manatunga finally announces: *I begin*.

But notice the word *tava prabhāvāt* — "by Your power." Manatunga is not claiming the inspiration. He is locating it where Jain doctrine locates everything: not in an external agent, but in the qualities one attends to. By attending carefully to the Jina's qualities, the writer's mind is transformed. The poem is not the writer's — it is what arises when the writer's mind sits with the subject for long enough.

Then the closing image — possibly the loveliest in all of Sanskrit poetry. A drop of water, falling onto the leaf of a lotus, takes on the appearance of a pearl. The water is ordinary. The leaf is the lotus. The transformation happens because of the surface, not the drop. The drop, by itself, is nothing. *On the lotus*, it shines.

Manatunga is saying: my words are the water drop. The Jina is the lotus leaf. Whatever beauty appears in this stotra is not from me. It is from the surface my words happen to land on.

After seven verses of preparation — bowing, acknowledging predecessors, admitting limits, naming the doe and the cuckoo — verse 8 is where Manatunga finally lifts the pen.

But even here, he does not claim the work. *Tava prabhāvāt* — by Your power. The words are not his; they are what arises when his attention rests long enough on what the Jina represents. He is the lotus leaf, not the water. The water drop is small, ordinary, easily overlooked — until it lands somewhere that can show what it might become.

This is one of the quietest moments in the stotra, and one of the most important. The verse models a specific kind of devotional creativity: the artist as host, not author. You provide the surface. You provide the attention. What comes is not yours. What comes is what happens between you and what you have been attending to.

For anyone making something — code, art, a difficult conversation — this verse is a release from the burden of having to *be* the source. The source is the subject. You only have to be the surface that lets the subject show.

That is why, by the end of this verse, Manatunga can finally begin. Not because he has overcome his small intellect. Because he has stopped trying to be the source.
