Tirthankaras are not gods

This is the single most important thing to get right about Jainism, and the one most often misunderstood.

When non-Jains see images of Mahavir or Adinath in temples — flowers offered before them, mantras recited, devotees bowing — the natural assumption is that these are deities being worshipped.

They are not. Jain doctrine is explicit and unwavering on this point.

A Tirthankara is a jina — a "conqueror." Someone who, in their final birth, attained complete liberation (moksha) through their own effort, by destroying the karma that bound their soul. Having attained liberation, they are no longer subject to rebirth. They reside in Siddhaloka at the apex of the universe.

What this means in practice: a Tirthankara cannot answer your prayer. Cannot grant your wish. Cannot intervene in the world. Cannot save you. They have completely transcended the world and have no further connection to it.

Why, then, do Jains have temples? Why bow before murtis? Why recite their names?

For the same reason a young pianist might keep a portrait of Bach over their practice room. The image is not Bach. Bach cannot help them play. But the image is a reminder of what is possible — what excellence looks like, what they are working toward.

Jain puja — and the recitation of stotras like the Bhaktamara — is veneration of an example, not solicitation of a deity. Acharya Manatunga's verses repeatedly clarify this. He does not ask Adinath for anything. He simply praises what Adinath represents.

This makes Jainism, in a specific technical sense, a non-theistic tradition. There is no creator god. There is no salvific god. Liberation comes from your own effort, walking the path the Tirthankaras showed.

For a young person trained to be skeptical of religion's promises — "pray and your wishes will be granted, fail to believe and be punished" — Jainism may turn out to be unexpectedly compatible with that skepticism. It does not promise what it cannot deliver. It only points the way.

So when you bow before a Tirthankara murti, or recite the Bhaktamara, you are not asking. You are acknowledging. You are saying: another human being walked the path to its end. The same path is open to me.
