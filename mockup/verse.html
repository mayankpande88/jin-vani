Verse One
भक्तामर-प्रणत-मौलि-मणि-प्रभाणा-
मुद्योतकं दलित-पाप-तमो-वितानम्।
सम्यक् प्रणम्य जिन-पाद-युगं युगादा-
वालम्बनं भव-जले पततां जनानाम्॥

bhaktāmara-praṇata-mauli-maṇi-prabhāṇām
udyotakaṁ dalita-pāpa-tamo-vitānam
samyak praṇamya jina-pāda-yugaṁ yugādā-
v ālambanaṁ bhava-jale patatāṁ janānām

Simple Meaning · सरल अर्थ

मैं भगवान आदिनाथ के चरणों में नमस्कार करता हूँ — जो देवों के मुकुट तक चमकते हैं, जो पाप के अंधकार को मिटा देते हैं, और जो इस दुनिया के दुःखों में डूबते लोगों के लिए एकमात्र सहारा हैं।

I bow to the feet of Bhagavan Adinath, the first Jina. His feet are so radiant that even the gods bow before him — their crowns shining brighter from his light. He is the only refuge for souls drowning in the ocean of worldly life.

Today · Why this matters now

Why bow to anyone?

The first verse of the Bhaktamara is a mangalacharana — an opening invocation. It begins with a bow.

In a generation taught to "be your own hero," opening with reverence for someone else's example can feel uncomfortable. Doesn't this just reinforce the hierarchies we're trying to escape?

But notice what the verse does not do.

The speaker doesn't ask Adinath for anything. No wealth, no protection, no favor, no afterlife reward. The verse simply acknowledges: someone walked this path before me. Their teaching is the only refuge in the ocean of worldly life.

This is closer to a scientist citing a predecessor than a believer praying. Standing on the shoulders of giants.

On this point the texts are clear: a Tirthankara is not a savior. Acharya Kundakunda's Samayasara teaches that the soul is its own doer (karta) and its own enjoyer (bhokta) — liberation comes through the soul's own purification, not from an outside agent.1 Acharya Pujyapada's Sarvarthasiddhi calls the Tirthankara the avalambana — the support, the example — not the giver of moksha.

What the Tirthankara is: an example. He is what is possible. Bowing acknowledges that another human being reached liberation through their own effort, and that the same path is open to anyone willing to walk it.

In Jainism, bhakti — devotion — is profound respect for someone who showed what is possible, and the willingness to walk in their footsteps. The Bhaktamara itself is the demonstration: across all 48 verses, Manatunga praises Adinath's qualities and asks for nothing.2 No request, no petition, no plea. The structure is recognition, not solicitation.

For a modern reader, perhaps this is the surprising part: a tradition often called "religious" with no creator god, no salvation through prayer, no intermediary between you and what you could become. Just you, your effort, and the example of those who went before.

That is what the first bow of the Bhaktamara is.

हिन्दी अनुवाद

जो देवों के झुके हुए मुकुट की मणियों की प्रभा को प्रकाशित करते हैं, जो पापरूपी अंधकार के समूह को नष्ट करते हैं, युग के आरम्भ में संसाररूपी समुद्र में डूबते हुए जीवों के लिये सहारा बने हैं — ऐसे प्रथम जिन (आदिनाथ) के दोनों चरणों को मैं भलीभाँति प्रणाम करके (स्तुति करता हूँ)।

भावार्थ

आचार्य मानतुङ्ग जी इस मङ्गलाचरण से स्तोत्र का प्रारम्भ करते हुए कहते हैं — इस संसार-समुद्र में जहाँ अनादि से जीव कर्मों के बोझ से डूबते रहे हैं, वहाँ केवल जिन-चरण ही आधार हैं। यहाँ "युगादि" शब्द युग के आदि-तीर्थंकर ऋषभदेव (आदिनाथ) के लिए विशेषरूप से प्रयुक्त हुआ है।

English Translation

Having truly bowed to the pair of feet of the first Jina — feet that illumine the radiance of the jewels in the crowns of bowing devotees, that dispel the spreading darkness of sin, that are the only refuge in the beginning of the era for those who fall into the ocean of worldly existence — (I now begin this hymn).

Meaning & Context

Acharya Manatunga opens the stotra with this mangalacharana — auspicious invocation — establishing both the addressee (Bhagavan Adinath, Rishabhanatha) and the central image: the Jina's feet as the only refuge for souls drowning in samsara.

The "jewels in crowns" refers to the devas themselves bowing — even celestials, for all their splendor, have nothing to offer the Jina but reverence. The word yugadi ("at the beginning of the era") is a specific epithet of Adinath, who founded the spiritual order of the present time-cycle.
