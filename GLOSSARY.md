# Glossary — Critical Terminology

**Purpose:** This document defines every Jain term used in product copy and content, and lists terms we explicitly will *not* use because they carry incorrect meanings in the Jain context. Religious precision is non-negotiable.

**Editorial focus:** This library is **Digambara-focused** (DECISIONS.md C-015). Where Digambara and Shvetambara usage differ, the Digambara form is primary in our product copy. Shvetambara forms are noted only where comparison is genuinely useful.

**Status:** Draft by Claude — flagged terms require Digambara cultural reviewer sign-off before publish.

**Confidence levels:**
- ✅ Verified — well-established usage, consistent across primary sources
- ⚠️ Flagged — usage I'm reasonably sure of but want reviewer confirmation
- ❓ Open — genuinely uncertain, must be resolved by reviewer

## Core terms — used in product copy

### Tirthankara ✅
A *ford-maker* (literally: one who builds a crossing across the ocean of *samsara*). A liberated soul who, in their last birth, establishes the four-fold *sangha* and shows the path to liberation. There are 24 Tirthankaras in the present time-cycle.

**Not:** "god," "deity," "avatar," "prophet," "messiah." Tirthankaras are not divine intermediaries; they have attained liberation themselves and shown the path.

### Jina ✅
*Conqueror* — one who has conquered the inner enemies (passions, ignorance). Synonymous with *Arihant* in most usage. Tirthankaras are Jinas; not all Jinas are Tirthankaras.

### Arihant ✅
One who has destroyed the four *ghati* karmas (knowledge-obscuring, perception-obscuring, deluding, obstructing) and attained omniscience while still embodied. Worshipped first in the Namokar Mantra.

### Siddha ✅
A liberated soul that has shed all karma and resides in *Siddhaloka* at the apex of the universe. The state after physical death of an Arihant.

### Acharya ✅
The head of a monastic order; a preceptor who has mastered the scriptures and guides monks, nuns, and laypeople.

### Upadhyaya ✅
A monk-teacher who teaches scripture to other monks.

### Sadhu / Muni ✅
A Jain monk who has taken the five great vows (*mahavrata*).

### Aryika ✅ (Digambara)
A female ascetic in the Digambara tradition. Aryikas wear a single white sari (unlike Digambara male monks who are unclothed). Aryikas are venerated as *upadhyaya*-level (teaching role) but according to traditional Digambara doctrine, full *moksha* in the present birth is held to require the male body and complete *digambara* state.

(In Shvetambara, the term *sadhvi* is used for nuns. We use *aryika* in this library since it is the Digambara term.)

### Shravak / Shravika ✅
A male / female lay follower of Jainism. Takes the twelve vows (*anuvrata* + *gunavrata* + *shikshavrata*) appropriate to a layperson.

### Moksha ✅
Liberation from the cycle of birth and death. The soul, freed of all karma, attains its pure nature.

**Not:** "salvation" (carries Christian theological baggage that doesn't apply).

### Kevala-jnana / Kevali ✅
Omniscience — direct, complete, unmediated knowledge of all substances in all their modes across all three times. A *kevali* is one who has attained this. Tirthankaras attain kevala-jnana before establishing the *sangha*.

### Samavasarana ✅
The divine assembly hall manifested for a Tirthankara's first sermon after attaining *kevala-jnana*. A specific iconographic and theological feature unique to Tirthankaras.

### Lanchhana ✅
The identifying symbol associated with each Tirthankara, depicted at the base of their *murti*. (e.g., bull = Rishabhanath, lion = Mahavir, snake-hood = Parshvanath.)

### Mudra ✅
Hand position / posture in iconography. Tirthankara murtis are typically shown in *padmasana* (lotus-seated, hands in *dhyana mudra*) or *kayotsarga* (standing meditation).

### Padmasana ✅
The lotus-seated meditative posture.

### Kayotsarga ✅
The standing meditative posture; literally "abandoning the body."

### Murti ⚠️
A sacred sculptural representation of a Tirthankara. Used reverentially.

**Not:** "idol" — pejorative connotation in English religious discourse, doesn't capture the role of *murti* in Jain practice. Always use *murti* or "sacred image."

### Puja ✅
Veneration ritual. In Jain context, *puja* honors the Tirthankara as an example to follow, not as a deity granting requests.

### Aarti ✅
A specific veneration ritual involving lights/lamps offered before a *murti*.

### Pratikraman ✅
The introspective ritual of confession, repentance, and reflection on one's conduct over a period (daily, fortnightly, etc.). Central daily practice.

### Ahimsa ✅
Non-violence in thought, speech, and action. The first and foundational vow.

### Aparigraha ✅
Non-attachment, non-possessiveness. One of the five great vows.

### Anekantavada ✅
The doctrine of *multiplicity of viewpoints* — reality has infinite aspects; truth statements are perspectival. A core philosophical principle.

### Syadvada ✅
The doctrine of *conditional predication* — every statement about reality is qualified by a conditional ("in some respect"). Often paired with *anekantavada*.

### Karma ✅
In Jain technical usage, karma is a substance — extremely subtle particles of matter (*pudgala*) that bind to the soul and cause its bondage. Note: this is a more substantive doctrine than the colloquial use of the word "karma" in Western popular discourse.

### Stotra ✅
A hymn of praise, typically composed in verse. Bhaktamara Stotra is an example.

### Sutra ✅
An aphoristic text. Tattvartha Sutra is an example. Also used for canonical scriptures (e.g., the *Anga* sutras of the Shvetambara canon).

### Sastra ✅
A treatise; a longer doctrinal or philosophical work.

### Purana ✅
A genre of narrative scripture covering mythological / cosmological / biographical content. *Adipurana* is an example.

### Charitra ✅
A biographical or hagiographical narrative. *Trishashti Shalaka Purusha Charitra* (Hemachandra) is the classic.

### Agama ✅
The canonical scriptures; what Mahavir and the Tirthankaras taught, transmitted through the *ganadharas* (chief disciples).

### Sangha ✅
The four-fold community: monks (*sadhu*), nuns (*sadhvi*), laymen (*shravak*), laywomen (*shravika*).

### Tapas / Tapasya ✅
Austerity, ascetic practice. Central to the Jain path of self-purification.

## Tradition names

### Digambara ✅ (this library's focus)
"Sky-clad" — the tradition where male monks (*muni*) practice complete nudity as the visible expression of total *aparigraha*. Holds that final *moksha* in the present birth requires the *digambara* state and that Mahavir did not marry. Strong in Karnataka, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Bundelkhand.

This library follows the Digambara tradition. (See DECISIONS.md C-015.)

**Not:** "sect." Use "tradition" or "*sampradaya*."

### Bispanthi ✅ (Digambara sub-tradition)
A Digambara sub-tradition that uses wet substances (water, flowers, fruit, sweets) in temple *puja*, venerates subordinate *yaksha*/*yakshini* deities alongside Tirthankaras, and accepts *Bhattaraka* administrative authority over temples. Common in older temples in Maharashtra and Karnataka.

### Terapanthi (Digambara) ✅
A Digambara reform sub-tradition emphasizing strict *adhyatmika* (introspective) practice, using only dry *ashtadravya* (eight prescribed dry substances) in *puja*, not venerating subordinate deities, and rejecting *Bhattaraka* authority over monks. Foundational figures include Pt. Banarsidas (17th c.) and Pt. Daulatram (author of *Chhah Dhala*). Common in North India, especially Rajasthan and the Hindi belt.

**Note:** The Digambara Terapanthi is **unrelated** to the Shvetambara Terapanthi (founded 1760 by Acharya Bhikshu). Different traditions, same name.

### Bhattaraka ✅
A Digambara religious-administrative figure, typically clothed in saffron, who heads a *peeth* (seat) and oversees temple administration. Bispanthi accepts Bhattaraka authority; Terapanthi (Digambara) rejects it. Notable seats include Shravanabelagola, Mudbidri, and Humbach.

### Shvetambara
"White-clad" — the other major Jain tradition. Monks and nuns wear white robes. Holds that women can attain *moksha* and that Mahavir did marry (per the *Kalpa Sutra*). Strong in Gujarat and Rajasthan. **This library does not present Shvetambara content as primary.**

### Sthanakvasi
A sub-tradition within Shvetambara that does not perform *murti* worship. Out of scope for this library.

## Words we explicitly do not use

Words to avoid in product copy and content, with reason:

| Avoid | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| God / deity | Tirthankaras are not divine; they're liberated teachers | Tirthankara, Jina, Arihant |
| Idol | Pejorative English connotation | *Murti*, sacred image |
| Salvation | Christian theological baggage | Liberation, *moksha* |
| Worship (in some contexts) | Implies prayer-for-favor relationship | Veneration, honor, *puja* (used precisely) |
| Pray to (a Tirthankara) | Tirthankaras don't grant prayers — they've left *samsara* | Venerate, recite, contemplate |
| Sect | Pejorative, suggests division from a "true" version | Tradition, *sampradaya* |
| Bible of Jainism | English-Christian framing | Scripture, *agama*, named text |
| Reincarnation as a "soul transmigrating" | Imprecise — soul carries karma, takes new body | Rebirth, transmigration of *jiva* in *samsara* |
| Founder of Jainism (about Mahavir) | Mahavir was the 24th Tirthankara, not the founder | The 24th Tirthankara, last Tirthankara of this time-cycle |
| Nirvana (interchangeably with *moksha*) ❓ | Both terms occur in Jain texts but have specific technical usage; reviewer to clarify | *Moksha* in most contexts |

## Names of major texts (consistent spelling we'll use)

To avoid spelling drift across the corpus. Phase 1 corpus per C-014:

- **Navkar Mantra** ⚠️ — also spelled *Namokar Mantra* / *Namaskar Mantra*. Working choice: **Navkar Mantra** (most common spoken usage in Digambara North India).
- **Chattari Mangalam** ⚠️ — Prakrit. Working choice as written.
- **Bhaktamara Stotra** — **48-verse Digambara version** (not the 44-verse Shvetambara recension). Composer: **Acharya Manatunga**. Spelling: not "Bhaktamar," "Bhaktambar."
- **Mangalashtak** ⚠️ — Digambara daily prayer; spelling working choice. Author: traditionally attributed to Acharya Pujyapada or anonymous.
- **Chhah Dhala** — Hindi devotional in 6 *dhalas* (sections). Author: **Pt. Daulatram Kasliwal** (18th c., Terapanthi Digambara). Spelling: not "Chha Dhala," "Chhe Dhala."
- **Tattvartha Sutra** — using the Digambara reading with *Sarvarthasiddhi* (Acharya Pujyapada's commentary) as authority. Composer: **Acharya Umaswami** (Digambara form; Shvetambara uses Umaswati — we use Umaswami).
- **Dravya Sangraha** — 58 gathas. Composer: **Acharya Nemichandra Siddhantachakravarti**.
- **Ratnakaranda Shravakachara** — Digambara lay ethics in 150 verses. Composer: **Acharya Samantabhadra** (2nd c.).
- **Samayasara** — 415 gathas total; Phase 1 covers selected key gathas. Composer: **Acharya Kundakunda** (2nd c.).
- **Ishtopadesh** — 51 gathas. Composer: **Acharya Pujyapada** (5th-6th c.).

**On the Bhaktamara verse count:** The 4 verses present in the Digambara 48-verse recension and absent from the Shvetambara 44-verse recension are traditionally associated with the *Manatunga* legend (the chains miraculously breaking on each invocation). Verify the canonical placement of these 4 verses in Sprint 2 against a recognized Digambara edition (e.g., Bharatiya Jnanpith or Mahavir Aradhana Kendra editions).

## Transliteration scheme

We use **IAST** (International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration) for romanized Sanskrit:
- Long vowels with macron: ā ī ū
- Retroflex consonants with under-dot: ṭ ḍ ṇ ṣ
- Palatal sibilant: ś
- Anusvara: ṃ; visarga: ḥ
- Sandhi shown faithfully

Reason: IAST is the academic standard, unambiguous, and what serious learners expect. We will *also* offer a simplified transliteration view for casual readers (e.g., "Bhaktamara" instead of "Bhaktāmara") — selectable in settings.

## Translation registers

When we say:
- **Translation** = literal rendering of meaning
- **Meaning / Bhavarth** = explanatory paraphrase, brings out the sense for a reader who doesn't share the text's assumed knowledge
- **Word-by-word** = each Sanskrit word with grammatical info and gloss; for serious learners
- **Note** = editorial commentary on context, allusion, or technical term

These four registers are kept distinct. We never blend translation with meaning silently.
